Zero Conditional: Variations & Extended Uses

Explore modals, imperative, and the difference between 'if' and 'when' in zero conditional structures.

Modals in the Result Clause

⚡ Expressing certainty, permission, or possibility

Using can, will, may:

In zero conditional, the result clause can include modals like can (permission/ability), will (certain future result), or may (possibility). The if-clause still uses present simple.

If you are over 18, you can vote. (permission)
If you don’t water plants, they will die. (certain result)
If the temperature drops, water may freeze. (possibility)

Imperative in the Result Clause

📢 Instructions, advice, and commands

Structure: If + present simple, (then) imperative

The imperative gives a direct instruction or advice based on the condition. It is common in user manuals, advice, and warnings.

If you feel tired, take a rest.
If the machine stops, press the reset button.
If you see smoke, call the fire department.

If vs When in Zero Conditional

🔁 Nuance of certainty

When to use each:

If is used for general truths or possible situations; when implies a situation that is certain or habitual. Both are often interchangeable in zero conditional, but "when" suggests a stronger sense of time/certainty.

If you heat ice, it melts. (general truth)
When you heat ice, it melts. (also correct, same meaning)
If I am late, I feel stressed. (possible situation)
When I am late, I feel stressed. (habitual, every time)
