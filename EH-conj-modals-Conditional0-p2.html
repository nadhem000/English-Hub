II. 1. Modals in the Result Clause

🔧 Using can, will, may

📘 In zero conditional, the result clause can include modals:

The if-clause still uses present simple. Modals like can (permission/ability), will (certain future result), or may (possibility) add nuance to the result.

✅ can — permission or ability:

If you are over 18, you can vote. (permission)

✅ will — certain future result:

If you don't water plants, they will die. (certain result)

✅ may — possibility:

If the temperature drops, water may freeze. (possibility)

If you are over 18, you can vote.
If you don't water plants, they will die.
If the temperature drops, water may freeze.

II. 2. Imperative in the Result Clause

📋 Structure: If + present simple, (then) imperative

📘 The imperative gives a direct instruction or advice based on the condition:

This structure is common in user manuals, advice, and warnings. The word "then" is optional before the imperative.

If you feel tired, take a rest.
If the machine stops, press the reset button.
If you see smoke, call the fire department.

II. 3. If vs When in Zero Conditional

🔄 When to use each

📘 If — general truths or possible situations:

If is used for general truths or possible situations. It presents a condition that may or may not occur.

📘 When — certain or habitual situations:

When implies a situation that is certain or habitual. It suggests a stronger sense of time/certainty, as if the condition always happens.

💡 Note:

Both are often interchangeable in zero conditional, but "when" suggests a stronger sense of time/certainty.

If you heat ice, it melts. (general truth)
When you heat ice, it melts. (also correct, same meaning)
If I am late, I feel stressed. (possible situation)
When I am late, I feel stressed. (habitual, every time)
