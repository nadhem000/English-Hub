Master the subjunctive 'were', modals 'could' and 'might', and advanced advice structures.

The Subjunctive 'Were' for All Persons

✨ Formal & hypothetical 'were'

Using 'were' instead of 'was':

In formal English and in hypothetical or unreal situations, were is used for all subjects (I, he, she, it, we, they) in the if-clause. This is called the subjunctive mood and adds formality or emphasis to the unreal condition.

If I were rich, I would buy a yacht. (not 'was')
If she were here, she would help us.
If they were younger, they would travel more.

Modals in the Result Clause: Could & Might

⚡ Expressing ability or possibility

Using could and might:

Instead of 'would', we can use could (ability or possibility) or might (weaker possibility) in the result clause. The if-clause still uses past simple.

If I spoke French, I could work in Paris. (ability)
If we left now, we might arrive on time. (possibility)
If he had a car, he could drive us to the station.

Expressing Advice: If I Were You...

💡 Giving hypothetical recommendations

Classic structure for advice:

The structure If I were you, I would... is a common way to give advice. It uses the subjunctive 'were' and 'would' in the result. This structure is always hypothetical and polite.

If I were you, I would accept the job offer.
If I were him, I wouldn't say that.
If I were her, I might study abroad.
