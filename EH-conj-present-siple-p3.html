Negative Forms in Simple Present

‚ùå How to Form Negative Sentences

Basic Negative Structure:

In English, we use the word "not" to form negative sentences in the simple present tense. The structure depends on the type of verb used in the sentence.

Types of Negative Sentences:
  • Negative with "To Be"
  • Negative with "To Have"
  • Negative with Other Verbs

Negative with "To Be"

üî§ Negative Formation with "To Be"

Step-by-Step Process:

With the verb "to be," we simply add "not" after the verb to form negative sentences.

Formula: [Subject] + [To Be] + "not" + [Rest of Sentence]

Subject Positive Negative
I I am a student. I am not a student.
You You are my friend. You are not my friend.
He He is a teacher. He is not a teacher.
She She is happy. She is not happy.
We We are here. We are not here.
They They are students. They are not students.
Contractions:

In spoken English and informal writing, we often use contractions (short forms) of "to be + not".

I'm not a student.
You aren't my friend. (or You're not my friend)
He isn't a teacher. (or He's not a teacher)
She isn't happy. (or She's not happy)
We aren't here. (or We're not here)
They aren't students. (or They're not students)

Negative with "To Have"

üî§ Negative Formation with "To Have"

Step-by-Step Process:

With the verb "to have," we use "do not" (don't) or "does not" (doesn't) to form negative sentences.

Formula: [Subject] + [Do/Does] + "not" + "have" + [Rest of Sentence]

Subject Positive Negative
I I have a car. I do not have a car.
You You have a book. You do not have a book.
He He has a cat. He does not have a cat.
She She has a dog. She does not have a dog.
We We have time. We do not have time.
They They have money. They do not have money.
Contractions:

In spoken English and informal writing, we often use contractions (short forms).

I don't have a car.
You don't have a book.
He doesn't have a cat.
She doesn't have a dog.
We don't have time.
They don't have money.

Negative with Other Verbs

üìù Negative Formation with Regular and Irregular Verbs

Step-by-Step Process:

For all other verbs (not "to be" or "to have"), we use "do not" (don't) or "does not" (doesn't) to form negative sentences.

Formula: [Subject] + [Do/Does] + "not" + [Base Form of Verb] + [Rest of Sentence]

Use "do not" for I, you, we, they. Use "does not" for he, she, it.

Subject Positive Negative
I I work every day. I do not work every day.
You You like coffee. You do not like coffee.
He He plays soccer. He does not play soccer.
She She studies English. She does not study English.
We We live in Paris. We do not live in Paris.
They They speak French. They do not speak French.
Contractions:

In spoken English and informal writing, we often use contractions (short forms).

I don't work every day.
You don't like coffee.
He doesn't play soccer.
She doesn't study English.
We don't live in Paris.
They don't speak French.
Negative with Irregular Verbs:

Even with irregular verbs, we still use "do not" or "does not" to form negatives.

I don't go to the gym. (go ‚Üí goes for he/she/it)
She doesn't do her homework. (do ‚Üí does for he/she/it)
They don't have classes today. (have ‚Üí has for he/she/it)

Passive Voice in Simple Present

üîÑ What is Passive Voice?

Definition:

In passive voice, the subject receives the action rather than performing it. We use passive voice when the doer of the action is unknown, unimportant, or obvious from context.

Structure of Passive Voice:

Formula: [Subject] + [am/is/are] + [Past Participle] + [by + agent (optional)]

Example: "The book is read by students."

Forming Passive Voice:

To form passive voice in simple present tense, use the appropriate form of "to be" (am, is, are) followed by the past participle of the main verb.

Active Voice Passive Voice
I write a letter. A letter is written by me.
You clean the room. The room is cleaned by you.
He fixes the car. The car is fixed by him.
She teaches the students. The students are taught by her.
We build houses. Houses are built by us.
They sell fruits. Fruits are sold by them.
Negative Passive Voice:

To form negative passive voice, add "not" after the form of "to be".

The book is not read by students.
The car is not fixed by him.
The students are not taught by her.
Question Form in Passive Voice:

To form questions in passive voice, invert the subject and the form of "to be".

Is the book read by students?
Is the car fixed by him?
Are the students taught by her?

Common Idioms in Simple Present

üí¨ What Are Idioms?

Definition:

Idioms are expressions whose meanings cannot be understood from the literal definitions of the words that make them up. They often use the simple present tense to express general truths or habitual actions.

Common Idioms in Simple Present:
Idiom Meaning Example
Break a leg Good luck "Break a leg on your performance tonight!"
Hit the books Study hard "I need to hit the books for my exam tomorrow."
Raining cats and dogs Raining very heavily "Take an umbrella; it's raining cats and dogs outside."
Once in a blue moon Very rarely "I only see my old school friends once in a blue moon."
Bite the bullet Endure a painful experience "I need to bite the bullet and tell my boss about the mistake."
Piece of cake Very easy "The test was a piece of cake; I finished in 20 minutes."
Using Idioms in Sentences:

Idioms are typically used in simple present tense to express general truths, habits, or common situations.

"When it rains, it pours." (When one bad thing happens, many bad things happen)
"The early bird catches the worm." (People who act early get advantages)
"Actions speak louder than words." (What you do is more important than what you say)
