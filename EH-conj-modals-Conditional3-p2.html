II. 1. Variations with 'Could Have'

💪 Expressing past ability or unrealized possibility

📘 Using could have in the result clause:

Instead of 'would have', we use could have to talk about ability or possibility in the past that did not happen. The if-clause remains in past perfect.

If I had studied more, I could have passed the exam. (ability)
If we had taken a taxi, we could have arrived earlier. (possibility)
She could have become a doctor if she had gone to university.

II. 2. Variations with 'Might Have'

🤔 Expressing weaker past possibility

📘 Using might have for uncertain outcomes:

Might have suggests a less certain alternative result. It shows something was possible but not definite.

If the weather had been better, we might have gone to the beach.
He might have forgotten if you hadn't reminded him.
If they had invited me, I might have attended.

II. 3. Expressing Criticism & Alternative Past Outcomes

💬 Using could have, should have, might have for criticism

📘 Structures for criticism or regret:

We often use could have, should have, or might have to criticize past actions or imagine better alternatives. Should have expresses strong criticism or regret.

You should have told me the truth. (criticism)
They could have been more careful. (alternative outcome)
She might have succeeded if she had tried harder.
We should have left earlier; we wouldn't have missed the flight.
