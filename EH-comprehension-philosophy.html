Reading Passage

Reading Time: 6-8 minutes

Several years have now passed since I first realized how many false opinions I had accepted as true in my youth, and how doubtful everything I subsequently built upon them was. I became aware that, if I wanted to establish anything firm and lasting in the sciences, I needed to demolish everything completely and start again from the foundations.

But the task seemed enormous, and I decided to wait until I was old enough to be sure that no better time for undertaking it would present itself. I have delayed so long that I would now be guilty if I used further indecision as an excuse to keep putting it off.

Today, then, I have rid my mind of all worries and arranged for myself a clear period of free time. I am here alone, and I will finally and seriously address myself to the upheaval of all my former opinions.

To achieve this, it will not be necessary to show that each of my beliefs is false – that may be impossible. But reason already convinces me that I should withhold my assent from opinions that are not completely certain and indubitable, just as carefully as I do from those that are obviously false.

So, for the purpose of rejecting all my opinions, it will be enough to find in each some reason for doubt. I need not examine them individually, because that would take forever. But since undermining the foundations will cause whatever is built upon them to collapse, I will attack the principles upon which all my former beliefs rested.

Whatever I have accepted until now as most true has come to me through my senses. But occasionally I have found that the senses deceive, and it is prudent never to trust completely those who have deceived us even once.

Yet although the senses sometimes deceive us about objects that are very small or distant, there are many other matters about which one cannot doubt them – for example, that I am here, sitting by the fire, wearing a winter dressing gown, holding this paper in my hands, and so on.

How could I deny that these hands and this entire body are mine? Unless perhaps I were to compare myself to some madmen whose brains are so impaired by a persistent dark vapor that they insist they are kings when they are destitute, or that they are dressed in purple when they are naked.

But such people are insane, and I would appear equally insane if I modeled my reasoning on theirs. Nevertheless, I must remember that I am a man, and that I am therefore accustomed to sleeping at night and having dreams in which I experience the same things that madmen do when awake – sometimes even more improbable things.

How often does my evening slumber persuade me of ordinary things – that I am here, dressed in my gown, seated by the fire – when in fact I am lying undressed between the sheets! At this moment, I certainly look at this paper with wide-awake eyes; I shake my head and it is not asleep.

But I see so clearly that there are no definitive signs by which to distinguish being awake from being asleep that I am quite astonished. My amazement is such that it is almost capable of persuading me that I am now dreaming.

From "Meditations on First Philosophy" by René Descartes (Meditation I)

Vocabulary Building

demolish
/dɪˈmɒlɪʃ/
To completely destroy or break down a structure or idea.
"Descartes wanted to demolish his old beliefs to build new knowledge."
indubitable
/ɪnˈdjuːbɪtəbəl/
Impossible to doubt; unquestionable.
"He sought indubitable truths that could serve as foundations for science."
prudent
/ˈpruːdənt/
Acting with or showing care and thought for the future.
"It is prudent not to trust those who have deceived us even once."
impair
/ɪmˈpeə/
To weaken or damage something, especially a mental faculty.
"Madmen have brains impaired by persistent dark vapor, says Descartes."
destitute
/ˈdestɪtjuːt/
Without money, food, or other basic necessities.
"A madman might claim he is a king when he is actually destitute."
