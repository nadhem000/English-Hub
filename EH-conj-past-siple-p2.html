Simple Past Tense: Questions & Adverbs

Learn to form questions and use adverbs of time correctly in the past

Forming Questions in Simple Past

‚ùì How to Form Questions

Basic Question Structure:

In the simple past tense, we use the auxiliary verb "did" to form questions for all subjects. The main verb stays in its base form.

Types of Questions:
  • Yes/No Questions (questions that can be answered with yes or no)
  • Wh- Questions (questions that begin with what, where, when, why, who, how)
  • Negative Questions

Questions with "To Be" in Past

üî§ Question Formation with "To Be" in Past

Step-by-Step Process:

With the verb "to be" in the past tense, we invert the subject and the verb to form questions. We don't use "did" with "to be".

Formula: [Was/Were] + [Subject] + [Rest of Sentence] + ?

Subject Statement Question
I I was a student. Was I a student?
You You were my friend. Were you my friend?
He He was a teacher. Was he a teacher?
She She was happy. Was she happy?
We We were here. Were we here?
They They were students. Were they students?
Wh- Questions with "To Be":

For Wh- questions, place the question word at the beginning, then follow with the inverted subject and verb.

Where were you yesterday?
What was your first job?
How was your vacation?

Questions with "To Have" in Past

üî§ Question Formation with "To Have" in Past

Step-by-Step Process:

With the verb "to have" in the past tense, we use "did" as the auxiliary verb to form questions.

Formula: [Did] + [Subject] + [Have] + [Rest of Sentence] + ?

Subject Statement Question
I I had a car. Did I have a car?
You You had a book. Did you have a book?
He He had a cat. Did he have a cat?
She She had a dog. Did she have a dog?
We We had time. Did we have time?
They They had money. Did they have money?
Wh- Questions with "To Have":

For Wh- questions, place the question word at the beginning, then follow with "did," subject, "have," and the rest of the sentence.

What did you have for breakfast?
Where did she have her party?
How many siblings did they have?

Questions with Other Verbs

üìù Question Formation with Regular and Irregular Verbs

Step-by-Step Process:

For all other verbs (not "to be" or "to have"), we use "did" as the auxiliary verb to form questions in the past tense.

Formula: [Did] + [Subject] + [Base Form of Verb] + [Rest of Sentence] + ?

Use "did" for all subjects (I, you, he, she, it, we, they).

Subject Statement Question
I I worked yesterday. Did I work yesterday?
You You liked the movie. Did you like the movie?
He He played soccer. Did he play soccer?
She She studied English. Did she study English?
We We lived in Paris. Did we live in Paris?
They They spoke French. Did they speak French?
Wh- Questions with Other Verbs:

For Wh- questions, place the question word at the beginning, then follow with "did," subject, base form of verb, and the rest of the sentence.

What did you do yesterday?
Where did he work last year?
How often did they exercise?
Questions with Irregular Verbs:

Even with irregular verbs, we still use "did" to form questions, and the main verb stays in its base form.

Did you go to the gym yesterday? (go ‚Üí went in statements)
Did she do her homework? (do ‚Üí did in statements)
Did they have classes yesterday? (have ‚Üí had in statements)

Adverbs of Time in Past

‚è±Ô∏è What Are Adverbs of Time?

Definition:

Adverbs of time tell us when something happened. They are commonly used with the simple past tense to specify when an action occurred.

Common Adverbs of Time in Past:
Adverb Meaning Example
yesterday the day before today I worked yesterday.
last week the week before this week She visited her grandmother last week.
last month the month before this month We moved last month.
last year the year before this year They graduated last year.
two days ago two days before today He called two days ago.
in 2010 during the year 2010 I was born in 2010.
Placement in Sentences:

1. Usually at the end of the sentence:

I went to the store yesterday.
She called me last night.
They arrived two hours ago.

2. Sometimes at the beginning for emphasis:

Yesterday, I went to the store.
Last year, we visited Paris.
Adverbs of Time in Questions:

In questions, adverbs of time usually come at the end of the question.

Did you work yesterday?
When did she arrive last night?
Where were you last week?
