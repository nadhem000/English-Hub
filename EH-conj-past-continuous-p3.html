Negative Forms in Past Continuous

‚ùå How to Form Negative Sentences

Basic Negative Structure:

In the past continuous tense, we form negative sentences by adding "not" after the form of "to be" (was/were).

Types of Negative Sentences:
  • Negative with "To Be"
  • Negative with "To Have"
  • Negative with Other Verbs

Negative with "To Be"

üî§ Negative Formation with "To Be"

Step-by-Step Process:

With the verb "to be" in past continuous, we add "not" after "was/were" to form negative sentences.

Formula: [Subject] + [Was/Were] + "not" + [Verb-ing] + [Rest of Sentence]

Subject Positive Negative
I I was studying English. I was not studying English.
You You were watching TV. You were not watching TV.
He He was working at the office. He was not working at the office.
She She was reading a book. She was not reading a book.
We We were playing soccer. We were not playing soccer.
They They were having dinner. They were not having dinner.
Contractions:

In spoken English and informal writing, we often use contractions (short forms) of "was/were + not".

I wasn't studying English.
You weren't watching TV.
He wasn't working at the office.
She wasn't reading a book.
We weren't playing soccer.
They weren't having dinner.

Subject Positive Negative

Even with irregular verbs, we still use the same negative structure in the past continuous tense.

Passive Voice in Past Continuous

üîÑ What is Passive Voice?

Definition:

In passive voice, the subject receives the action rather than performing it. We use passive voice when the doer of the action is unknown, unimportant, or obvious from context.

Structure of Passive Voice:

Formula: [Subject] + [was/were] + "being" + [Past Participle] + [by + agent (optional)]

Example: "The book was being read by students."

Forming Passive Voice:

To form passive voice in past continuous tense, use the appropriate form of "to be" (was/were) followed by "being" and the past participle of the main verb.

Active Voice Passive Voice
I was writing a letter. A letter was being written by me.
You were cleaning the room. The room was being cleaned by you.
He was fixing the car. The car was being fixed by him.
She was teaching the students. The students were being taught by her.
We were building houses. Houses were being built by us.
They were selling fruits. Fruits were being sold by them.
Negative Passive Voice:

To form negative passive voice, add "not" after "was/were".

The book was not being read by students.
The car was not being fixed by him.
The students were not being taught by her.
Question Form in Passive Voice:

To form questions in passive voice, invert the subject and "was/were".

Was the book being read by students?
Was the car being fixed by him?
Were the students being taught by her?

Common Past Continuous Idioms

üí¨ What Are Idioms?

Definition:

Idioms are expressions whose meanings cannot be understood from the literal definitions of the words that make them up. They often use the past continuous tense to express actions that were in progress in the past.

Common Past Continuous Idioms:
Idiom Meaning Example
Raining cats and dogs Raining very heavily "It was raining cats and dogs when I left home."
Beating around the bush Avoiding the main topic "He was beating around the bush instead of giving a direct answer."
Burning the candle at both ends Working too hard "She was burning the candle at both ends to finish her project."
Hitting the hay Going to bed "I was hitting the hay when you called last night."
Pulling my leg Joking with me "I thought you were pulling my leg when you said you won the lottery."
Spilling the beans Revealing secret information "He was spilling the beans about the surprise party."
Using Past Continuous Idioms in Sentences:

Past continuous idioms are typically used to describe actions that were in progress in the past.

"It was raining cats and dogs when I was driving to work yesterday."
"She was burning the candle at both ends to prepare for her exams."
"He was pulling my leg when he said he could fly."
