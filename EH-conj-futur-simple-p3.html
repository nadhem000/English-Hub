Simple Future Tense: Negative, Passive & Idioms

Learn to form negative sentences, use passive voice, and understand common idioms in the future tense

Negative Forms in Simple Future

‚ùå How to Form Negative Sentences

Basic Negative Structure:

In English, we use the word "not" to form negative sentences in the simple future tense. The structure is the same for all verbs.

Types of Negative Sentences:
  • Negative with "Will"
  • Negative with "Going To"
  • Negative with All Verbs

Negative with "Will"

üî§ Negative Formation with "Will"

Step-by-Step Process:

With "will," we add "not" after "will" to form negative sentences.

Formula: [Subject] + "will not" + [Base Form of Verb] + [Rest of Sentence]

Subject Positive Negative
I I will study tomorrow. I will not study tomorrow.
You You will call me later. You will not call me later.
He He will arrive soon. He will not arrive soon.
She She will finish the work. She will not finish the work.
We We will travel next week. We will not travel next week.
They They will buy a car. They will not buy a car.
Contractions:

In spoken English and informal writing, we often use contractions (short forms) of "will not".

I won't study tomorrow.
You won't call me later.
He won't arrive soon.
She won't finish the work.
We won't travel next week.
They won't buy a car.

Negative with "Going To"

üî§ Negative Formation with "Going To"

Step-by-Step Process:

With "going to," we add "not" after the appropriate form of "to be" (am, is, are).

Formula: [Subject] + [am/is/are] + "not" + "going to" + [Base Form of Verb] + [Rest of Sentence]

Subject Positive Negative
I I am going to study tomorrow. I am not going to study tomorrow.
You You are going to call me later. You are not going to call me later.
He He is going to arrive soon. He is not going to arrive soon.
She She is going to finish the work. She is not going to finish the work.
We We are going to travel next week. We are not going to travel next week.
They They are going to buy a car. They are not going to buy a car.
Contractions:

In spoken English and informal writing, we often use contractions (short forms).

I'm not going to study tomorrow.
You aren't going to call me later.
He isn't going to arrive soon.
She isn't going to finish the work.
We aren't going to travel next week.
They aren't going to buy a car.

Negative with All Verbs

üìù Negative Formation with Regular and Irregular Verbs

Step-by-Step Process:

For all verbs, we use "will not" or "am/is/are not going to" to form negative sentences in the future tense.

Formula 1: [Subject] + "will not" + [Base Form of Verb] + [Rest of Sentence]

Formula 2: [Subject] + [am/is/are] + "not" + "going to" + [Base Form of Verb] + [Rest of Sentence]

Subject Positive (Will) Negative (Will)
I I will work tomorrow. I will not work tomorrow.
You You will like the movie. You will not like the movie.
He He will play soccer. He will not play soccer.
She She will study English. She will not study English.
We We will live in Paris. We will not live in Paris.
They They will speak French. They will not speak French.
Negative with Irregular Verbs:

Even with irregular verbs, we still use "will not" or "am/is/are not going to" to form negatives, and the main verb stays in its base form.

I won't go to the gym tomorrow. (go ‚Üí will go in statements)
She won't do her homework. (do ‚Üí will do in statements)
They won't have classes tomorrow. (have ‚Üí will have in statements)

Passive Voice in Simple Future

üîÑ What is Passive Voice?

Definition:

In passive voice, the subject receives the action rather than performing it. We use passive voice when the doer of the action is unknown, unimportant, or obvious from context.

Structure of Passive Voice:

Formula: [Subject] + [will be] + [Past Participle] + [by + agent (optional)]

Example: "The book will be read by students."

Forming Passive Voice:

To form passive voice in simple future tense, use "will be" followed by the past participle of the main verb.

Active Voice Passive Voice
I will write a letter. A letter will be written by me.
You will clean the room. The room will be cleaned by you.
He will fix the car. The car will be fixed by him.
She will teach the students. The students will be taught by her.
We will build houses. Houses will be built by us.
They will sell fruits. Fruits will be sold by them.
Negative Passive Voice:

To form negative passive voice, add "not" after "will".

The book will not be read by students.
The car will not be fixed by him.
The students will not be taught by her.
Question Form in Passive Voice:

To form questions in passive voice, invert the subject and "will".

Will the book be read by students?
Will the car be fixed by him?
Will the students be taught by her?

Common Future Tense Idioms

üí¨ What Are Idioms?

Definition:

Idioms are expressions whose meanings cannot be understood from the literal definitions of the words that make them up. They often use the simple future tense to express future plans or predictions.

Common Future Tense Idioms:
Idiom Meaning Example
Cross that bridge when we come to it Deal with a problem when it happens "We'll cross that bridge when we come to it."
Hit the road Begin a journey "We'll hit the road early tomorrow morning."
Turn over a new leaf Start behaving better "I'll turn over a new leaf starting next month."
Bite the bullet Endure a painful experience "I'll bite the bullet and tell my boss tomorrow."
Go the extra mile Make extra effort "I'll go the extra mile to finish this project on time."
Jump on the bandwagon Join a popular activity "I think I'll jump on the bandwagon and try that new app."
Using Future Tense Idioms in Sentences:

Future tense idioms are typically used to describe future plans, decisions, or predictions.

"I'll cross that bridge when I come to it."
"We'll hit the road as soon as the sun rises."
"She'll turn over a new leaf starting next week."
