Going Deeper: Nuance & Comparison

⚖️ Choosing the right modal

Why nuance matters:

The choice between can, could, and be able to can change the meaning, tone, or time reference of a sentence. This section explores those subtle differences.

Key ideas in part 2:
  • Comparing can, could, and be able to (ability, time, specificity).
  • Degrees of politeness – from standard to very formal.
  • Secondary uses: possibility with can, could, might.

Comparing Can, Could, and Be Able To

🔍 How are they different?

ModalUsageExample
canpresent ability, general possibility, informal permissionI can swim. / It can rain here.
couldpast ability, polite request, less certain possibilityI could run fast as a child. / Could you help?
be able tospecific ability (especially past/future), after other modals, infinitiveI was able to finish on time. / You will be able to drive.
Key point:

Use was/were able to for specific past achievements (not general ability). For general past ability, both could and was/were able to are possible.

Degrees of Politeness & Formality

🤝 How tone changes with modals

LevelExample (request/permission)Context
StandardCan you open the window?everyday, neutral
PoliteCould you open the window?more polite, common
Very polite / formalWould you mind opening the window? / May I open the window?formal situations
Examples:
Can I borrow your pen? (informal)
Could I borrow your pen? (more polite)
May I borrow your pen? (very formal)

Expressing Possibility: Can, Could, Might

🌤️ Degrees of certainty

ModalCertaintyExample
cangeneral / theoreticalIt can be very cold in winter. (general truth)
couldspecific but not certain (50%?)It could rain later. (possible)
mightless certain (30%?)It might rain, but I doubt it. (uncertain)
The weather can be unpredictable here. (general)
This could be the solution. (specific possibility)
She might be at home. (less sure)
