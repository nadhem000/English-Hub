II. 1. Choosing the Right Modal

🤔 Why nuance matters

The choice between can, could, and be able to can change the meaning, tone, or time reference of a sentence. This section explores those subtle differences.

Key ideas in part 2:
  • Comparing can, could, and be able to (ability, time, specificity).
  • Degrees of politeness – from standard to very formal.
  • Secondary uses: possibility with can, could, might.

II. 2. Comparing Can, Could, and Be Able To

🔍 How are they different?

Modal Usage Example
can present ability, general possibility, informal permission I can swim. / It can rain here.
could past ability, polite request, less certain possibility I could run fast as a child. / Could you help?
be able to specific ability (especially past/future), after other modals, infinitive I was able to finish on time. / You will be able to drive.

💡 Key point: Use was/were able to for specific past achievements (not general ability). For general past ability, both could and was/were able to are possible.

II. 3. Degrees of Politeness & Formality

🎩 How tone changes with modals

Level Example (request/permission) Context
Standard Can you open the window? everyday, neutral
Polite Could you open the window? more polite, common
Very polite/formal Would you mind opening the window? / May I open the window? formal situations
Can I borrow your pen? (informal)
Could I borrow your pen? (more polite)
May I borrow your pen? (very formal)

II. 4. Expressing Possibility: Can, Could, Might

🌦️ Degrees of certainty

Modal Certainty Example
can general / theoretical It can be very cold in winter. (general truth)
could specific but not certain (50%?) It could rain later. (possible)
might less certain (30%?) It might rain, but I doubt it. (uncertain)
The weather can be unpredictable here. (general)
This could be the solution. (specific possibility)
She might be at home. (less sure)
