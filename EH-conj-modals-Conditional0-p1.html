Zero Conditional: Introduction & Basic Form

Learn how to express general truths, scientific facts, and habitual actions using the zero conditional.

Introduction to Zero Conditional

🔰 What is the Zero Conditional?

Definition:

The zero conditional is used to talk about things that are always true: general truths, scientific facts, and habitual actions. The result is certain and automatic.

Core ideas:
  • General truths: If you heat ice, it melts.
  • Scientific facts: If water reaches 100°C, it boils.
  • Habitual actions: If I am tired, I drink coffee.
  • "When" can replace "if" with no change in meaning: When you heat ice, it melts.
Examples:
If you freeze water, it becomes ice.
If it rains, the ground gets wet.
When I am late, my boss gets angry.
If people eat too much, they gain weight.

Basic Structure

📐 Form: If + Present Simple, Present Simple

Rule:

Both clauses use the present simple tense. The order can be reversed with no change in meaning. A comma is used when the if-clause comes first.

Structure Example
If + present simple, + present simple If you heat ice, it melts.
Present simple + if + present simple Ice melts if you heat it.
Using "when":

In zero conditional, "when" is often interchangeable with "if" because the result is always true.

When you heat ice, it melts.
When it rains, the grass grows.
Negative Form:

Use "don't" or "doesn't" in the verb phrase.

If you don't water plants, they die.
Water doesn't freeze if it's above 0°C.

Common Uses: General Truths & Habits

🌍 General Truths & Scientific Facts

Zero conditional is used for universal truths that are always valid.

If you drop an object, it falls to the ground.
If you mix red and blue, you get purple.

🔄 Habitual Actions

It also describes routines or habits that happen regularly.

If I feel stressed, I listen to music.
When he has a day off, he goes hiking.
