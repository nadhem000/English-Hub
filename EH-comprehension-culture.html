Cultural Differences

Intermediate Level Reading Comprehension

Level: B1-B2 (Intermediate)

Reading Passage

Reading Time: 4-5 minutes

Every country has its own unique customs and traditions. When people travel or move to another country, they often experience culture shock – the feeling of being confused or uncomfortable because things are different. Understanding cultural differences can help us avoid misunderstandings and build better relationships with people from other backgrounds.

One important cultural difference is greetings. In many Western countries like the United States, people often shake hands when they meet for the first time. In some European countries, close friends might kiss each other on both cheeks. However, in Japan, people bow to show respect. The depth of the bow depends on the person's social status. In Thailand, people press their hands together in a prayer-like gesture called the "wai" and slightly bow their head.

Personal space is another area where cultures differ. In North America, people usually stand about an arm's length away from each other during conversations. In many Latin American and Middle Eastern countries, people stand much closer when talking. If a North American backs away, it might seem rude to someone from a culture that prefers closer contact.

Attitudes toward time also vary greatly. In Germany, Switzerland, and Japan, punctuality is extremely important. Being late for a meeting is considered disrespectful. However, in countries like Mexico, Brazil, and Saudi Arabia, time is viewed more flexibly. Arriving 15 or 30 minutes late might be perfectly acceptable in social situations. People from these cultures often prioritize relationships over strict schedules.

Dining customs also reflect cultural values. In China and many Asian countries, it is polite to serve food to others before serving yourself. In India, many people eat with their right hand because the left hand is considered unclean. In Western cultures, people use forks, knives, and spoons. Making slurping noises while eating noodles is acceptable and even a compliment to the chef in Japan, but considered rude in many other countries.

Gift-giving customs provide another fascinating example. In China, it is polite to refuse a gift a few times before finally accepting it. The recipient does not open the gift in front of the giver to avoid seeming greedy. In contrast, in Western cultures, people usually open gifts immediately and express thanks. In some Middle Eastern cultures, it is important to use both hands when giving or receiving a gift to show respect.

Learning about cultural differences does not mean one way is right and another is wrong. Different cultures have developed different solutions to everyday social situations. By being open-minded and respectful, we can learn from each other and appreciate the rich diversity of human customs around the world.

Vocabulary Building

Culture shock
/ˈkʌltʃər ʃɒk/
The feeling of being confused or anxious when visiting a different culture
"After moving to Japan, Maria experienced culture shock because everything was different."
Customs
/ˈkʌstəmz/
Traditional and usual ways of behaving in a particular society
"It is important to learn local customs before traveling to another country."
Punctuality
/ˌpʌŋktʃuˈæləti/
The habit of being on time; not arriving late
"Punctuality is highly valued in German business culture."
Bow
/baʊ/
To bend your head or body forward as a sign of respect
"In Japan, it is customary to bow when meeting someone for the first time."
Recipient
/rɪˈsɪpiənt/
A person who receives something
"The recipient of the gift should show appreciation."
