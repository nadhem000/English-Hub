III. 1. Common Mistakes with May & Might

⚠️ Frequent errors

❌ Using 'may' for past possibility without 'have'

Incorrect: He may left early. ✅ Correct: He may have left early. (past possibility requires "have + past participle")

❌ Confusing 'may' and 'might' in reported speech

Incorrect: She said she may come. (if reporting a past statement) ✅ Correct: She said she might come. (shift back in reported speech)

❌ Using double modals

Incorrect: He might can help. ✅ Correct: He might be able to help. or He can help.

❌ Overusing 'may' for permission in informal contexts

While "May I?" is correct, in casual speech it can sound too formal. Can I get a coffee? is more natural among friends.

Additional Common Pitfalls
  • Using 'may' and 'might' interchangeably in probability: 'might' often suggests a lower probability.
  • Forgetting to backshift in reported speech with past tense reporting verbs: She thought she may be late. ❌ → She thought she might be late. ✅
  • Confusing 'maybe' (adverb) with 'may be' (modal + verb): Maybe he is late. (adverb = perhaps) vs He may be late. (modal verb expressing possibility).

III. 2. Idiomatic Expressions with May/Might

📚 Common fixed phrases

ExpressionMeaningExample
may/might as wellno reason not to; it's equally goodWe may as well start now.
if I maypolite way to interject or ask permissionIf I may, I'd like to add something.
be that as it maynevertheless; despite thatBe that as it may, we must continue.
might I sayhesitant or polite introductionMight I say you look lovely tonight?
may wellquite possiblyShe may well be the best candidate.

III. 3. Contextual Practice: At a Business Meeting

💼 Using modals for polite suggestions & formal requests

Chair: "Shall we begin? If I may, I'd like to call the meeting to order."
Anna: "Certainly. Might I suggest we review the budget first?"
Chair: "That's a good idea. May I ask everyone to turn to page 3?"
Ben: "If I may interject, I think there may be an error in the projections."
Chair: "Thank you, Ben. Be that as it may, we need to move forward. Could we vote?"
Anna: "We might as well decide today."

Note: Notice the use of may, might, and idioms for polite interaction.
