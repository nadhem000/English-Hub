II. 1. Nuance & Comparison

🎯 Choosing between may and might

Why nuance matters:

While may and might are often interchangeable, the choice can signal different levels of certainty, formality, or time. This section explores those subtle distinctions.

Key ideas in part 2:
  • Comparing may, might, and may/might have (past possibility).
  • Degrees of politeness – from can to may to might.
  • Secondary uses: concessive may, unreal might, might as well.
Nuance in Likelihood

May expresses about 50% certainty; might expresses about 30% certainty. Might often sounds more hesitant or tentative, making it common in polite suggestions. In negative forms, may not and might not can both express uncertainty, but might not often implies a slightly stronger doubt.

Time Reference

May and might are both used for present and future possibility. For past possibility, we use may have or might have. Might have can also indicate a missed opportunity (unreal past).

II. 2. Comparing May, Might, and Past Forms

ModalUsageExample
maypresent/future possibility (≈50% certainty), formal permissionIt may rain. / May I leave?
mightless certain possibility (≈30%), tentative suggestion, unrealIt might rain. / You might try this.
may have / might havepast possibility (may have = possible, might have = less certain / unreal)She may have left. / He might have missed the train.

Key point: In past possibility, might have can also express that something was possible but did not happen (unreal). He might have called, but he didn't.

II. 3. Additional Nuances & Degrees of Politeness

May vs Might in Questions

When asking about possibility, might is often preferred for politeness: Might it rain later? May is rarely used in questions about possibility except in formal contexts. For permission, may is the formal choice: May I come in?
