Going Deeper: Nuance & Comparison

⚖️ Choosing between may and might

Why nuance matters:

While may and might are often interchangeable, the choice can signal different levels of certainty, formality, or time. This section explores those subtle distinctions.

Key ideas in part 2:
  • Comparing may, might, and may/might have (past possibility).
  • Degrees of politeness – from can to may to might.
  • Secondary uses: concessive may, unreal might, might as well.

Nuance in Likelihood

May expresses about 50% certainty; might expresses about 30% certainty.

Might often sounds more hesitant or tentative, making it common in polite suggestions.

In negative forms, may not and might not can both express uncertainty, but might not often implies a slightly stronger doubt.

Time Reference

May and might are both used for present and future possibility.

For past possibility, we use may have or might have.

Might have can also indicate a missed opportunity (unreal past).

Comparing May, Might, and Past Forms

ModalUsageExample
maypresent/future possibility (≈50% certainty), formal permissionIt may rain. / May I leave?
mightless certain possibility (≈30%), tentative suggestion, unreal situationsIt might rain. / You might try this.
may have / might havepast possibility (may have = possible, might have = less certain / unreal)She may have left. / He might have missed the train.
Key point:

In past possibility, might have can also express that something was possible but did not happen (unreal). He might have called, but he didn't.

Additional Nuances

May have is used when the past possibility is considered fairly likely; might have suggests greater doubt or an unrealised outcome.

In questions about past possibility, we usually use might have rather than may have: Might she have forgotten?

May vs Might in Questions

When asking about possibility, might is often preferred for politeness: Might it rain later?

May is rarely used in questions about possibility except in formal contexts.

For permission, may is the formal choice: May I come in?

Using May/Might for Permission vs Possibility

May I…? is strictly a permission request.

It may rain is a possibility statement.

Might I…? can be used for very polite permission requests, but it is less common.

Common Mistakes with May/Might

  • Using may for past possibility without have: ❌ She may left. ✅ She may have left.
  • Using might to give permission: ❌ You might go now. ✅ You may go now. (Might expresses possibility, not permission.)
  • Overusing may in informal speech for permission – can or could are more natural in everyday contexts.

Secondary Uses: Concession, Unreal, and Idioms

UsageExampleExplanation
Concessive (may...but)He may be rich, but he isn't happy.admitting a fact before contrast
Unreal situation (might)If you tried, you might succeed.hypothetical, less direct
Might as wellWe might as well start.no reason not to, suggesting

May well / might well: expresses strong probability. Might just: slight but possible chance. May / might (just) as well: suggests alternative with same result.

May and Might in Conditionals

In real conditionals, may expresses possible result: If you study, you may pass.

In unreal conditionals, might is used: If you studied, you might pass.

May is rarely used in unreal conditionals.

May and Might for Wishes and Hopes

May is used in formal expressions of hope: May you have a safe journey.

Might can express a wish in more tentative or literary style: Might you find peace.
