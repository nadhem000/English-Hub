Must & Have to: Core Concepts & Primary Uses

Learn how to use must and have to to express obligation, necessity, and deduction

Introduction to Must & Have to

🔰 What are modals "must" and "have to"?

Definition:

"Must" and "have to" are used to express obligation, necessity, and deduction. "Must" is a modal verb; "have to" is a semi-modal that behaves like a normal verb in questions and negatives.

Core ideas:
  • Must – strong internal obligation, necessity, logical deduction.
  • Have to – external obligation (rules, laws), general necessity.
Examples:
I must go. (obligation)
You have to wear a uniform. (external obligation)
She must be at home. (deduction)
They have to work hard. (necessity)

Basic Grammar Rules

⚙️ Structure

Form Rule Example
Affirmativesubject + must / have to + base verbShe must study. / We have to leave.
Negativesubject + must not/mustn't / do/does not have to + base verbYou mustn't smoke. / He doesn't have to come.
QuestionMust + subject + base verb? / Do/Does + subject + have to + base verb?Must I go? / Do they have to pay?
Important:

Must has no past form (use "had to" for past). "Have to" uses auxiliary do/does for questions and negatives.

Primary Uses of "Must"

✅ 1. Strong internal obligation

We use must to express what the speaker feels is necessary (personal conviction).

I must call my mother.
You must see that film; it's amazing.

🧠 2. Deduction / logical conclusion

Must expresses that we are almost sure something is true based on evidence.

He's been driving for hours; he must be tired.
The lights are on. She must be home.

⚠️ 3. Prohibition (mustn't)

Must not / mustn't is used to say something is forbidden.

You mustn't smoke in the hospital.
We mustn't be late.

Primary Uses of "Have to"

🏛️ 1. External obligation (rules, laws)

We use have to when the obligation comes from an external source (law, rules, someone else).

Employees have to wear an ID badge.
You have to have a ticket to enter.

📆 2. General necessity

Have to expresses that something is necessary in everyday life.

I have to eat breakfast every day.
She has to take this medicine.

🔓 3. No obligation (don't have to)

Don't/doesn't have to means it is not necessary (but you can if you want).

You don't have to come if you're busy.
He doesn't have to work on Sundays.
